Alan Mathison Turing was elected a Fellow at King's College, Cambridge in 1935; in 1936, he proposed a general model of logic machines known as the Turing machine; in 1938, he received his PhD from Princeton University; in 1939, he began working for the British military, accelerating the Allied victory in World War II; in 1946, he was awarded the Order of the British Empire; from 1945 to 1948, he was in charge of research on automatic computing engines at the National Physical Laboratory in Teddington, London; in 1948, he became a senior lecturer at the University of Manchester and assistant to the head of the Automatic Digital Computer project; in 1949, he became the deputy director of the Computer Laboratory; in 1950, he proposed the possibility of machines possessing thought and the concept of the Turing Test; in 1951, he was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society; in 1954, he died after ingesting an apple containing cyanide at the age of 41.
